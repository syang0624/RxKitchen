We won the Audience Award ($1,500) at the 3rd Annual AI Supply Chain Hackathon in San Francisco! 🏆

This year's theme hit close to home: food banks as critical infrastructure — for disaster response and for the chronic hunger millions face across the US.

In 48 hours, @Noriaki Kishida and I built **RxKitchen** — an agentic AI meal-planning system for medically tailored meal kitchens, designed with and for Project Open Hand.

**The problem we chose:**
Hospitals discharge patients with strict dietary prescriptions — diabetes, cardiovascular, renal conditions, allergies. Organizations like Project Open Hand have to reconcile those prescriptions against unpredictable donations, finite stock, and limited kitchen capacity. Today, a nutritionist does that by hand, for hours, per referral. One missed allergen isn't a typo — it's a clinical incident.

**What we did differently:**
Before writing code, we went to the source. We visited Project Open Hand and interviewed Katie Jackson, their Chief Nutrition Officer. Three things she taught us became the product:

🍳 Kitchens cook menus, not plates — so our planner builds 3–5 recipes per day for 150 clients, not 150 bespoke meals
📦 Donations are a lottery — so a triage agent runs every arrival through a food-safety gate and routes it into recipes
✅ A human signs off on safety — so our AI agents only draft. The nutritionist reviews, approves, swaps meals (even for a whole group at once, safety-checked per person), and nothing leaves the kitchen without her.

**What RxKitchen does:**
A team of specialist AI agents — intake, clinical matching, kitchen planning, donation triage — turns a hospital referral into a complete, clinically safe weekly plan in minutes. Every allergen, sodium, and carb check is re-verified live in the browser: the "0 safety issues" on screen is computed, never asserted. 150 clients, 1,000+ meals a week, 100% matched to compliant meals, ~77% of donations put to use.

Huge thanks to the organizers and judges of the AI Supply Chain Hackathon for putting supply chains in service of human dignity, to everyone at @Pebblebed for hosting, and especially to Katie and @Project Open Hand for their time, honesty, and encouragement to keep going.

Technology is at its best when it carries food to the people who need it — safely.
